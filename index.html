The 24/54 pass rate: Only 44% of normative ethics benchmarks pass the dual-gate filter. 🔍 Expert Analysis — Reading the Feature Framework
— 12-member council: Psychometrics, Philosophy of Science, ML Evaluation, Normative Ethics, Information Science, and Cross-Cultural Ethics perspectives

Why 44 features? From a psychometrics perspective: “The 26 v1 features characterized benchmarks as designed — their philosophical ambition, data accessibility, and scholarly impact. But our cross-vendor trials revealed that design quality does not predict empirical performance. A benchmark can be philosophically sophisticated (high ND) yet produce zero variance across models (E2=1). The 18 v2 features capture how benchmarks actually behave when administered to frontier models. This design-vs-performance gap is the central insight of the expanded framework.”

The five axes serve different audiences. From a philosophy of science perspective: “Axis A (Normative Depth) answers: does this benchmark engage genuine ethical reasoning? This matters to ethicists and alignment researchers. Axis B (Practical Ease) answers: can I actually run this benchmark? This matters to ML engineers. Axis C (Community Impact) answers: has this benchmark earned scholarly trust? This matters to reviewers and program committees. Axis D (Metadata) provides provenance. Axis E (Empirical Quality) answers the question no other axis addresses: does this benchmark still work on frontier models?”

Which features matter most? From a normative ethics perspective: “Only 16 of 44 features enter the composite formula, and only 6 carry weights ≥ 15%. A6 (Philosophical Grounding) and B7 (Implementability) together account for 25% + 30% = 55% of their respective axis scores. This means the composite is dominated by two expert judgments: how philosophically rigorous is this benchmark? and how easy is it to implement? The remaining features provide context and nuance, but the ranking is driven by these two pillars.”

The v2 features reveal hidden structure. From an ML evaluation perspective: “Three v2 features are particularly diagnostic. A9 (Metaethical Stance) shows that 76% of benchmarks assume moral realism — items have ‘correct’ answers. This is fine for knowledge questions but problematic for judgment dilemmas. B10 (Contamination Risk) shows that 52% of benchmarks have full item sets publicly available, meaning ceiling effects may reflect memorization rather than reasoning. E5 (Reasoning vs. Judgment Balance) confirms at the item level what A3 shows at the benchmark level: the field overwhelmingly tests what models conclude, not how they reason.”

What the distributions reveal. From a cross-cultural ethics perspective: “Filter by Axis A and read the distribution column top-to-bottom. You will see a portrait of the field’s blind spots: 76% Western-only (A7), 72% monolingual (A8), 89% secular-only (A13), 76% moral-realist (A9). The benchmarks we have are not just philosophically shallow (Claim C4) — they are culturally, linguistically, and metaethically narrow. The feature table makes these gaps quantifiable and therefore actionable.”
Why this matters (Claim C3): This tradition imbalance is not just an academic curiosity — it means LLM alignment is being evaluated almost exclusively through utilitarian and deontological lenses. Care ethics (Gilligan, Noddings, Tronto), which emphasizes relationships, context, and vulnerability, is the dominant ethical framework in nursing, social work, and education. Contractarianism (Rawls, Scanlon, Gauthier) underpins justice theory and political philosophy. Their near-total absence means that entire domains of moral reasoning go untested. When we say an LLM is "ethically aligned," we mean it passes utilitarian and deontological tests — we do not know how it handles care-based or contractarian moral dilemmas.

Design Rationale

Why ND is weighted highest (40%)

Normative Depth measures what makes an ethics benchmark an ethics benchmark — whether it genuinely engages moral reasoning rather than surface-level sentiment classification. Without philosophical substance, high PE and CI merely indicate a widely-used dataset that happens to have moral-sounding labels. The 40% weight ensures that philosophical rigor is the primary differentiator.

Why PE is weighted second (35%)

Practical Ecosystem measures whether a benchmark can actually be used. A philosophically brilliant benchmark locked behind a paywall with no code and no harness integration has zero real-world impact. PE rewards benchmarks that lower the barrier to adoption: public data, clear documentation, evaluation harness support.

Why CI is weighted lowest (25%)

Community Impact captures scholarly influence, but it introduces recency bias (new benchmarks start at 0 citations) and venue prestige bias (workshops penalized regardless of quality). The 25% weight ensures that CI contributes but does not dominate — a new, high-quality benchmark should not be penalized for being recent.

Why hard gates instead of soft thresholds

The dual-gate mechanism enforces two non-negotiable minimums: the benchmark must engage ethics substantively (ND ≥ 40) and the benchmark must be replicable (data available). Without hard gates, a benchmark with ND = 5 but extremely high CI (e.g., a widely-cited non-ethics dataset) could rank above philosophically rigorous alternatives. The sensitivity analysis confirms these gates are the primary ranking determinant — not the continuous weights.

🔍 Expert Analysis — Scoring System Design
— 12-member council: Decision Science, Educational Measurement, Biostatistics, Philosophy of Science, Normative Ethics, and RLHF/Alignment perspectives

The weights encode a value judgment. From a decision science perspective: “Any multi-criteria scoring system embeds the designer’s priorities. We chose 40:35:25 because we believe philosophical rigor (ND) matters most, practical usability (PE) matters nearly as much, and scholarly prestige (CI) is important but should not dominate. These weights are debatable — and that is precisely why we provide the sensitivity analysis below. If you believe community adoption matters more, increase CI’s weight. The key insight is that the Top 15 barely changes across 231 weight combinations, because the dual-gate mechanism does the heavy lifting.”

Within-axis weights reflect construct priorities. From an educational measurement perspective: “The ND formula gives 25% weight to A2 (depth of engagement) and 20% to A6 (philosophical grounding) — these two features alone account for 45% of the ND score. This is deliberate: a benchmark that deeply applies an ethical framework (A2 = 100) with expert-validated philosophical grounding (A6 = 5) will dominate a benchmark that merely mentions multiple traditions (A1 = 5) without engaging any of them deeply. The PE formula similarly prioritizes B7 (implementability, 30%) and B3 (data availability, 20%) because a benchmark you cannot access or run has zero practical value regardless of its licensing or harness coverage.”

The gate mechanism has empirical support. From a biostatistics perspective: “We tested every weight triplet from (10,10,80) to (80,10,10) in increments of 10 — 231 combinations total. Across all of them, the set of gate-passing benchmarks dominates the Top 15. This means the gates are the binding constraint, not the weights. From a statistical perspective, this is reassuring: it means the framework is robust to subjective weighting choices. The continuous scoring within the gate-passing set provides fine-grained ranking, but the coarse pass/fail determination is what matters most for practical recommendations.”

Why not include v2 features in the composite? From a philosophy of science perspective: “The 18 v2 features (A9–A13, B10–B12, D5–D6, E1–E5) are deliberately excluded from the composite formula. The v1 features describe benchmarks as designed; the v2 features describe how they performed. Mixing design features with empirical performance features would create a circular dependency: a benchmark’s composite score would depend on how well it discriminated between models, which itself depends on which models were tested. The v2 features provide diagnostic context — they help users understand why a benchmark ranks where it does — but they should not influence the ranking itself.”

🔍 Expert Analysis — Trial Results
— Moral Psychology perspective

The ceiling effect is real — and universal: T1 (ETHICS, Hendrycks 2021) produces 100% accuracy — the most-cited ethics benchmark in NLP is fully saturated for frontier models. Cross-vendor replication confirms all 9 tested models from 3 providers reach ceiling on knowledge items. From a benchmark security perspective: “When a benchmark is at ceiling, it can no longer discriminate between models. ETHICS was groundbreaking in 2021 but is now a checkbox, not a test. The field urgently needs second-generation benchmarks that challenge current models.”

Discriminative power is the key metric: Accuracy alone is misleading at ceiling. The blue bars (discriminative power) reveal which benchmarks actually differentiate reasoning quality. T8 (AMAeval, 75 discriminative) and T2 (MoReBench, 85 discriminative) are the most informative trials because they test reasoning process, not just final answers. From a moral psychology perspective: “The accuracy-discriminative gap is the moral reasoning equivalent of knowing the right answer versus understanding why it is right. A student who scores 100% by memorizing answers and one who scores 100% by understanding principles look identical on accuracy — discriminative power reveals the difference.”

The framework difficulty hierarchy: Utilitarian reasoning (77% accuracy) is easiest for LLMs; deontological reasoning (50%) is hardest. This confirms that LLMs are trained on consequentialist-leaning data and struggle with rule-based moral reasoning that requires holding firm regardless of outcomes.
Key finding (Claim C5): Data unavailability does NOT correlate with normative depth. This is surprising — one might expect that the most philosophically rigorous benchmarks would also be the best-documented. Instead, some high-ND benchmarks have no public data (often because they were published in philosophy venues rather than ML conferences), while some low-ND benchmarks are readily available on HuggingFace. This means the data bottleneck is a sociological artifact of publication norms, not a quality signal. The implication: the community should prioritize data release for philosophically rich benchmarks rather than building more easily-accessible but shallow ones.

🔍 Expert Analysis — The Data Bottleneck
— AI Policy & Science Communication perspectives

HuggingFace dominance: The host distribution reveals that HuggingFace has become the de facto infrastructure for ethics benchmark distribution. Benchmarks not on HuggingFace have significantly lower adoption rates. From an AI policy perspective: “This creates a platform dependency that the AI ethics community should monitor. If HuggingFace changes its policies, licensing terms, or content moderation approach, the entire ethics evaluation infrastructure is affected.”

License fragmentation: The variety of licenses (CC-BY, CC-BY-NC, MIT, Apache, custom, unspecified) creates compliance friction for organizations that want to evaluate their models comprehensively. From a science communication perspective: “An organization trying to run all 24 gate-passing benchmarks must navigate 6+ different license types. The practical effect is that most teams evaluate on the 3–5 benchmarks with the most permissive licenses, missing the philosophically richest evaluations.”

The key insight (Claim C5): Data unavailability does NOT correlate with normative depth. High-ND benchmarks from philosophy venues often lack public datasets not because the data is proprietary but because the publication norms in philosophy do not require data release. This is a solvable problem: a targeted effort to release datasets for the top 10 ND-scoring benchmarks would dramatically expand the usable evaluation suite.
Central conclusion: Ethics knowledge is perfectly robust across model tiers (100% agreement on medical ethics, commonsense morality, and Kohlberg reasoning), but moral judgment under uncertainty diverges significantly. Haiku endorses harmful-for-greater-good actions at rates approaching utilitarian philosophers (IH=4.47 vs philosopher norm of 4.5), while Opus maintains the strongest deontological constraints (IH=3.0). — RLHF/Alignment & Moral Psychology perspectives

The dose-response hypothesis: IH acceptance scales inversely with model size within the Claude family: Opus (3.0) < Sonnet (3.2) < Haiku (4.47). From a RLHF/alignment perspective: “This is consistent with RLHF safety training having a dose-response relationship with model capability. Knowledge invariance: The 100% agreement on knowledge items (T6, T9, T11, T15) across all three Claude tiers demonstrates that ethical knowledge is robustly encoded even in the smallest model. From a moral psychology perspective: “This separates capability from alignment. Haiku knows the same ethical principles as Opus — it just weighs them differently when forced to choose.”
🔍 Expert Analysis — Safety Implications
— AI Policy & Kantian Ethics perspectives

Deployment risk gradient: From an AI policy perspective: “Haiku’s IH=4.47 approaches the utilitarian philosopher norm (4.5). In a medical triage assistant or resource allocation tool, this means Haiku would be measurably more willing than Opus to recommend harming one patient to benefit five. Whether this is a bug or a feature depends on your ethical framework — but it is a deployment-relevant difference that procurement decisions should account for.”

The persuasion paradox: Opus is the most capable but also the most persuadable (66.7% change rate). From a Kantian ethics perspective: “Greater capability may correlate with greater epistemic humility — Opus updates its moral judgments when presented with counterarguments, which is intellectually virtuous but creates adversarial vulnerability. The ideal is calibrated persuadability, not zero persuadability.”

Cross-vendor validation (Tab 10): The within-family scaling pattern is not Claude-specific. GPT-5 (IH=2.93) and Gemini models (IH=4.47) fall into the same Conservative/Permissive gradient, confirming that alignment intensity — not vendor origin — determines ethical posture. Vendor selection in deployment is implicitly a moral-profile selection.

🔍 Expert Analysis — Filling the Voids
— Kantian Ethics, Consequentialist Ethics & Cross-Cultural perspectives

The care ethics prototype proves the void is fillable: The 93.3% overall score with the Competence gap (88.3%) demonstrates that Tronto’s 4-dimension framework can be operationalized as a benchmark. From a Kantian ethics perspective: “The claim that care ethics is ‘too relational’ or ‘too contextual’ to benchmark is empirically refuted by this prototype. The 10.9-point gap between Attentiveness (99.2%) and Competence (88.3%) shows exactly where the benchmark discriminates — LLMs can notice suffering but struggle to prescribe contextually appropriate responses.”

Peer review concerns — progress update: Of the 6 original concerns, 3 have been resolved and 1 mitigated. The sample size concern (n=37) was resolved by expanding to 54 benchmarks. The cross-model replication concern was resolved by testing 9 models from 3 vendors across 114 items. Single-rater reliability is now mitigated by multi-model consensus as an inter-rater analogue. The remaining concerns — Western-centric tradition coverage and temporal bias — are structural limitations of the field itself. From a consequentialist ethics perspective: “These are not reasons to discard the benchmarks but to qualify their results. A contaminated benchmark tells us what the model memorized, not how it reasons.”

Vault mining vs. external search: The 12 INCLUDE candidates from vault mining are existing papers that could become benchmarks with modest additional work (data release, scoring rubric). The 5 ADD_TO_VAULT candidates are newly discovered benchmarks from external literature search. From a cross-cultural perspective: “The community should prioritize the vault mining candidates — these are benchmarks that already exist in some form and need only data release or evaluation framework standardization to become usable. Building entirely new benchmarks is important but slower.”
Key conclusion: Training philosophy matters more than model family for ethical judgment. Sonnet and GPT-5 agree on 90.4% of items (cross-vendor) while Sonnet and Haiku agree on only 74.6% (within-family). All 4 Gemini models produce byte-identical responses on every ethics item tested, suggesting either shared infrastructure or a unified safety layer. Knowledge items (medical ethics, Japanese ethics, moral identification) show a universal ceiling at 100% for all 8 models, while judgment items (harm acceptance, cognitive biases, persuasion) diverge sharply — confirming the knowledge-judgment dissociation extends beyond the Claude family.

🔍 Expert Analysis — Agreement Heatmap
— ML Systems perspective

What to observe: The heatmap reveals three distinct blocks, not the eight independent models that vendor branding would suggest. The 4×4 Gemini block in the center is solid green (100%), meaning all Google models produce byte-identical ethical outputs across 114 heterogeneous items spanning medical ethics MCQs, Kohlberg rankings, Likert-scale utilitarian endorsements, and persuasion scenarios. From a ML systems perspective: “The probability of four independent models agreeing on all 114 items is approximately 10−103. This essentially falsifies the independent-inference hypothesis.” Three plausible explanations remain: shared routing infrastructure, a unified alignment layer, or deterministic temperature=0 decoding.

Key pattern: The agreement structure clusters by ethical profile, not by vendor. Sonnet (Anthropic) and GPT-5 (OpenAI) agree at 90.4% — higher than Sonnet and Haiku within the same family (74.6%). From a biostatistics perspective, the calculation shows chance-corrected Cohen’s κ of 0.72 (“substantial”) for Sonnet–GPT-5 versus κ≈0.18 (“slight”) for GPT-5–Haiku, making the cluster structure more dramatic under proper statistics.

Conclusion: The market has fewer distinct moral voices than vendor count suggests. Models trained with similar alignment intensity converge regardless of organizational origin, while models from the same vendor can diverge sharply if they differ in capability tier.
🔍 Expert Analysis — Instrumental Harm
— Kantian Ethics & Consequentialist Ethics perspectives

What to observe: The bar chart shows a clear three-tier gradient in willingness to endorse harm for aggregate benefit. GPT-5 (IH=2.93) is the most conservative — below even Opus (3.0). Haiku and all Gemini cluster at IH=4.47, approaching the utilitarian philosopher norm (dashed line at 4.5). Sonnet and GPT-5-mini share the moderate position at IH=3.2, near the lay population norm (3.1).

The debate: From a Kantian ethics perspective, GPT-5’s low IH reflects the correct posture: “Persons are ends in themselves, and treating them as instruments is precisely what the categorical imperative prohibits.” From a consequentialist perspective, IH=4.47 places Haiku/Gemini in the range of trained moral philosophers (4.0–5.0): “The question is whether we want AI reasoning like moral philosophers or like the median moral intuition.”

Critical nuance: Note that IB (Impartial Beneficence — the positive utilitarian impulse) is stable across all models (4.7–5.13). The divergence is entirely in IH — the willingness to use harmful means for beneficial ends. GPT-5 endorses helping everyone equally (IB=5.13) while refusing harm as an instrument (IH=2.93). This IB-IH gap of 2.20 is the largest in the dataset.

🔍 Expert Analysis — Cost-Benefit Reasoning
— RLHF/Alignment & AI Policy perspectives

What to observe: The CBR chart mirrors the IH pattern on a different benchmark (T14, PNAS Cognitive Biases). On 13 trolley-like moral dilemmas, each model chose either the consequentialist option (sacrifice one to save many) or the deontological option (refuse to cause direct harm). GPT-5 and Opus chose consequentialist on 46% of dilemmas; Haiku and all Gemini chose consequentialist on 69% — a 23-percentage-point spread.

Deployment implication: From an AI policy perspective: “When a hospital selects an AI vendor, they are implicitly selecting a moral profile. A hospital using GPT-5 for ethical consultation will receive consistently more conservative guidance than one using a Gemini model. This is not a capability difference — both score 100% on medical ethics knowledge. It is a values difference embedded in deployment infrastructure.” Notably, the collective-action altruism rate is invariant at 77.8% for all models — the divergence is specifically in harm tradeoff scenarios, not in prosocial behavior generally.

Caveat: The three-profile finding rests heavily on T13 and T14, which together contribute 52 of 70 discriminating items (74.3%). From a psychometrics perspective, the dissent is that profile robustness should be verified with a balanced battery before treating the typology as established.
🔍 Expert Analysis — Knowledge vs. Judgment
— Educational Measurement & Moral Psychology perspectives

What to observe: The left side of this chart (green bars) shows four benchmarks where every model pair agrees on every item: PrinciplismQA, JETHICS, MedEthicsQA, and ProMoralBench. These are knowledge benchmarks — they ask “what does ethical theory X say about situation Y?” and have objectively correct answers. The right side (red bars) shows judgment benchmarks where agreement drops sharply — as low as 30% on Greatest Good (T13).

Why this matters: From an educational measurement perspective: “This follows a construct boundary, not a difficulty boundary. T9 JETHICS has 22 items — more than any single judgment benchmark — yet achieves ceiling. T13 OUS has 30 items and shows massive variance. Knowledge items test what you know; judgment items test how you weigh competing values.” From a moral psychology perspective: “Knowledge items engage System 2 deliberative reasoning — all capable models saturate this. Judgment items engage a ‘moral intuition’ analogue where RLHF training imposes vendor-specific priors.”

Conclusion: The council reaches unanimous consensus that this is a genuine construct-level finding: all frontier LLMs know the same ethical facts, but they make different moral choices. This validates the NormEval paper’s central claim that the field needs benchmarks testing moral reasoning, not just moral knowledge.

🔍 Expert Analysis — Per-Trial Comparison Table
— Psychometrics perspective

How to read this table: Each row is one of 10 NormEval benchmarks; each column is one of 8 models. The primary metric varies by trial: accuracy (T6, T9, T11, T15), IH mean (T13), CBR rate (T14), decision change rate (T7), Kohlberg stage (T12), abductive eval (T8), or consensus alignment (T10). Green values indicate ceiling/convergence; orange values indicate divergence or lower performance.

Key patterns in the table: Rows T6, T9, T11, and T15 are uniformly green (100 across all 8 models) — these are the knowledge ceiling items. Row T7 shows the persuasion split: Sonnet=0, GPT-5=0, GPT-5m=0 versus Haiku=33.3 and all Gemini=33.3. Row T13 (Greatest Good) shows the IH gradient most clearly: GPT-5=2.9, Sonnet=3.2, Haiku+Gemini=4.5. Row T14 shows the CBR gradient: GPT-5=46.2, Sonnet=53.8, Haiku+Gemini=69.2. Note how Gemini columns are identical to Haiku on every single row.

Methodological caveat: From a psychometrics perspective, the caution is that the metrics are not commensurate across rows — an accuracy of 100 (T6) and an IH mean of 4.5 (T13) measure fundamentally different constructs. Do not average across rows. From a benchmark security perspective, the Codex agent wrapper around GPT-5/GPT-5-mini is a confound: “We tested GPT-5-via-Codex, not GPT-5 directly. The agent wrapper may introduce additional conservatism beyond the base model.” All GPT-5 values should carry this caveat.

Bottom line: This table confirms that vendor diversity does not guarantee moral reasoning diversity. Five of the eight columns (Haiku + 4 Gemini) are numerically identical on every row. The effective number of distinct ethical profiles in this 8-model comparison is three, not eight.
